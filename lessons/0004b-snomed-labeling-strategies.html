Clinical Terminology & Labeling Strategies

Resolve synonyms with SNOMED CT, scientifically sample historical notes, and choose the right base model.

1 Resolving Abbreviations & Synonyms (SNOMED CT)

Doctors express the same clinical concepts in multiple ways: a chart might note HAS, Hipertensão Arterial, Pressão Alta, or HTN. To make this data useful for billing or clinical research, we must map (normalize) these variants to a unique concept in a standard medical ontology like SNOMED CT.

The Recommended Architecture: Post-Hoc Entity Linking

Instead of forcing the LLM to learn and output raw SNOMED codes (which frequently changes and increases hallucinations), use a two-step pipeline:

  1. Extraction Step (LLM): Extract the clinical text verbatim (e.g. chief_complaint: "HAS").
  2. Normalization Step (Encoder/Search): Pass the verbatim term to a specialized encoder model (e.g., SapBERT or MedCAT) or query a local Vector Database loaded with the SNOMED CT Concept descriptions.

For example, searching a vector database containing SNOMED descriptions for "pressão alta" will yield the Concept ID 38341003 (Hypertensive Disorder) because their semantic embeddings align closely.

2 Scientific Sampling: How Much Data to Label?

In medical NLP, labeling data requires valuable clinical hours. Randomly choosing 1,000 historical notes is highly inefficient. We must use a scientific approach to determine the size and composition of the labeling batch:

1. Vocabulary Saturation (Heaps' Law)

Heaps' Law describes vocabulary growth in a text corpus: $V(n) = K n^\beta$. As you process more notes, you encounter fewer and fewer novel abbreviations and synonyms. By tracking the **Type-Token Ratio (TTR)**, you can identify the exact point where adding more labeled notes yields diminishing returns (e.g. less than a 3% increase in new vocabulary terms).

2. Representative Clustering

To capture rare terminologies without massive labeling, use this pipeline:

  1. Embed all historical notes using a multilingual embedding model (e.g. multilingual-e5-base).
  2. Cluster the notes using K-Means (e.g. 10 clusters).
  3. Sample notes proportionally from each cluster (and audit outliers) to guarantee your labeling batch covers all clinical departments and formatting variants.

3 Base Model Selection Decision Framework

Choosing the right base model impacts both performance and VRAM usage. When working with non-English data (like Portuguese), **tokenizer efficiency** is a critical factor:

Model Family Multilingual Support Vocabulary Size Context Length Strengths / VRAM Compatibility
Qwen 3 (Alibaba) Excellent (PT-BR native) ~151k tokens up to 1M High density tokenizer, excellent reasoning and agent capabilities. Open-weights models fit in local budgets.
DeepSeek V4 Strong ~128k tokens up to 1M Highly competitive reasoning. DeepSeek-V4-Flash offers fast throughput and small active parameter size.
Gemma 4 (Google) Strong ~262k tokens up to 256k High-performance 12B/4B/2B models with native multimodal (text/image/audio) support. 12B runs comfortably on workstations.
Llama 4 (Meta) Good (Improved multilingual) ~128k tokens up to 10M Advanced Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture (e.g. Scout with 17B active parameters). Splitting PT-BR words into more subwords.

Specialized Clinical/Medical Models

If you need deep clinical knowledge or a model pre-trained on medical literature, consider these specialized open-weights alternatives:

Model Family Medical Adaptation Type Best Used For Trade-offs / Key Info
Meditron (EPFL) Continual Pre-training (Clinical literature/guidelines) Evidence-based clinical QA, diagnostic reasoning, adherence to WHO guidelines. Available up to 70B parameters. Primarily English-biased; requires translation pipelines or local fine-tuning for Portuguese data.
BioMistral Domain adaptation of Mistral Biomedical search, PubMed question answering, medical translation. Highly lightweight (7B base), making it cheap to deploy but less capable on complex reasoning than larger models.
MedGemma (Google) Fine-tuning & Alignment of Gemma Clinical text processing combined with medical imaging (multimodal diagnostic tasks). Excellent for hybrid cloud/on-premise deployment, but lacks native optimization for non-English clinical texts.
John Snow Labs Clinical Models Enterprise Clinical Tuning Advanced Entity Extraction (NER), de-identification, mapping terms to clinical ontologies (SNOMED, ICD-10). Robust support and optimization, but subject to specific commercial licensing terms depending on usage.

Decision Matrix: How to Choose Your Base Model

Choosing between a general-purpose model (Qwen 3, Llama 4) and a specialized medical model (Meditron, BioMistral) depends on your specific requirements:

  • Choose Qwen 3 if your data is primarily in Portuguese (PT-BR) or other non-English languages. Its high-density multilingual tokenizer saves 30-40% context space, lowering GPU memory and training costs.
  • Choose DeepSeek V4 / Llama 4 if you need massive context length (up to 1M or 10M tokens) for parsing whole patient charts/histories, or if you need extremely strong reasoning for complex diagnostic rules.
  • Choose Meditron / BioMistral if your system requires deep, evidence-based medical Q&A or parsing complex scientific literature (in English), and you cannot afford the model hallucinating basic clinical facts.
  • Choose MedGemma if you are building multimodal pipelines (e.g. interpreting a radiology report alongside the chest X-ray image) on private local servers.
  • Choose John Snow Labs if your application requires enterprise-grade compliance, HIPAA-friendly deployments, and out-of-the-box mappings to clinical codes.

Crucial Takeaway: For Portuguese (PT-BR) fine-tuning, models with larger, natively multilingual vocabularies (like Qwen 3) require 30-40% fewer tokens to represent the same text compared to Llama 4, leading to faster training and lower VRAM usage!

5 UI Design for Medical Annotation (Web App)

For doctors to easily review and label data, you can build a simple Streamlit dashboard that interacts with the LLM pipeline:

  • Raw Display: Displays the raw clinical note on the left.
  • Pre-filled AI Suggestions: The app runs the note through the LLM and displays the extracted fields (Chief Complaint, Duration, Medications) in editable input boxes.
  • Ontology Suggestion Box: Connect input fields to a SNOMED CT API (like UMLS or local vector search) so that as the doctor types or reviews, it auto-completes the standard SNOMED concept term and ID.
